# Sweep agent 05 — Green Space & Waterside
Research date: 2026-04-11
Total areas covered: 95
Agent focus: T2.4 — named, walk-time-verified green and water coverage per area, with honest judgement on whether a park or waterway is core to the area's identity or merely nominal proximity.

## Methodology

Every area was assessed against a stated notional centre (typically the primary station or a well-known square), with walk minutes estimated via Google Maps pedestrian routing and cross-checked against OpenStreetMap park polygons and borough council parks pages. For rivers, canals, and docks I used OpenStreetMap waterways plus the Thames Path / Regent's Canal / Grand Union Canal / Lee Navigation references from canalrivertrust.org.uk and Wikipedia. Park acreage comes from Wikipedia or borough parks directories; Royal Parks data is from royalparks.org.uk directly. The central question I am trying to answer for the consensus pool is not "is there a green space somewhere near this area" — almost every London area has one — but **"when a resident of this area walks out of their front door on a Sunday morning, is a park or waterway part of the lived experience, or is it a map feature they never actually use?"**.

I tier green/water identity four ways:

| Tier | Meaning | Examples |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Core** | The feature defines the area. You cannot describe the area without mentioning it. | Little Venice, Rotherhithe, Clapham Common, Hampstead Heath edges, Tide linear park at Greenwich Peninsula, Hackney Wick |
| **Strong** | Residents use the feature multiple times a week; it shapes routines and listings. | Battersea Park, Stoke Newington, Mile End, Kidbrooke Village, Herne Hill, Bethnal Green |
| **Nominal** | A park is within 10–15 min walk and listings mention it, but it is not central to lived experience. | Whitechapel, Acton, Archway, Camberwell, Walworth |
| **Absent** | No meaningful green or water within 15 min; what exists is too small or low-quality to count. | Aldgate East, Old Street core, Stepney Green core, parts of Shepherd's Bush |

The Core distinction matters because T2.4 is a tick-box in the rubric but T3 identity is a differentiator: a Core water/green area earns double weight — once on the basic inventory and again on identity.

Per-area format below: notional centre; inventory of named features with walk minutes and citations; identity call; T2.4 verdict with confidence; sources.

### greenwich-peninsula — Greenwich Peninsula
**Centre:** North Greenwich station (Jubilee). **Inventory:** Tide linear park — 5 km elevated riverside walk by Diller Scofidio + Renfro, phased from 2019, 1–5 min from every Greenwich Peninsula BTR [5][6]; Central Park ~6 acres, 5 min S [7]; Peninsula / Ecology Park 15-acre wetland managed by Trust for Urban Ecology, 8 min SE — one of London's best inner-urban wildlife reserves [8]; Thames frontage wraps three sides of the peninsula, continuous Thames Path [9]. **Identity:** Core water (Thames) + Core designed green (Tide). Every building fronts or is 5 min from the river. The Tide is genuinely one of London's most ambitious recent public realm deliveries and is free, public, and residential-adjacent. One of the strongest green-and-water identities in the dataset. **T2.4:** Pass — strong. T3 differentiator warranted. Confidence: **high**.

## Cross-cutting findings

**1. Dock identity is the strongest T3 differentiator in the dataset.** Canary Wharf, Canada Water, Surrey Quays / Rotherhithe, Royal Docks, Shadwell, Limehouse, and Greenwich Peninsula all have preserved dock basins as a defining feature. These areas consistently outperform non-dock equivalents on lived-green-and-water experience because a dock basin delivers what a small park cannot: a continuous large water surface visible from multiple buildings, walkable around, and providing real ecological habitat (birds, fish) plus recreational use (sailing, kayaking, open-water swimming). The dock inventory is the cleanest "T2.4 Pass with T3 identity uplift" signal in the dataset. Synthesis should weight dock presence heavily.

**2. Canal presence varies from nominal to defining.** The Regent's Canal specifically is a different-class feature when it passes through an area in a way that shapes the built form — King's Cross, Camden Town, Mile End, Bow, Hackney Wick, Little Venice — versus merely running near an area (Angel, Bethnal Green partially). The distinction is whether the canal is the design spine or a convenient walk. The Grand Union Canal has the same bifurcation (Little Venice / Paddington Basin vs Hayes). Synthesis should separate "canalside neighbourhood" from "within 15 min walk of canal" — they are different rubric scores.

**3. Elizabeth Line areas south of the Thames do NOT automatically lose river access.** The special instruction in the brief anticipated this but reality is more nuanced. Canary Wharf, Woolwich, Custom House / Royal Docks all have river + dock access immediately; Whitechapel does not (Thames 20 min S); Abbey Wood does not (the Thames is 15 min N but blocked by road and rail); Farringdon and Bond Street are central. The generalisation that holds is that **south-of-Thames Elizabeth Line stations lose river access unless they were specifically built on the river** (Woolwich Arsenal, Canary Wharf, Custom House). Synthesis should not apply a blanket rule.

**4. Green-and-water is highly correlated with desirability for young professionals when the feature is real, but nominal proximity is worth almost nothing.** Clapham Common, Hampstead Heath edges, London Fields, Peckham Rye, Brockwell Park, Clissold Park, Little Venice, Rotherhithe, Hackney Wick — these areas all have the green/water feature as part of lived routine. Residents move *because of* the feature. Whitechapel, Aldgate, Old Street, Shepherd's Bush, North Acton, Cricklewood — these have parks within 15 min but the parks are not part of lived experience. The T2.4 rubric row should almost certainly weight "core identity" vs "nominal proximity" rather than just counting walk minutes.

**5. Royal Parks (Hyde, Kensington Gardens, Regent's, Green, St James's) are structurally different from council parks.** When an area is Royal-Park-adjacent — Marylebone, St John's Wood, Belsize Park (via Primrose Hill), Camden Town, South Kensington, Notting Hill — the T2.4 score should be maximal because Royal Parks are maintained at a higher standard, stay open later, are safer after dark, and deliver the largest continuous green surfaces in central London. Hampstead Heath (City of London managed, not a Royal Park) is in the same category by quality. Synthesis should recognise Royal Park adjacency as a distinct tier above council-park adjacency.

**6. Water identity suppression by area reputation.** Shadwell, Limehouse, Deptford, Woolwich all have genuinely strong river / dock features that are under-weighted in popular perception because the areas have rough reputations. The water is real, the green is real, but the T1 safety signal has masked T2.4 quality. Synthesis should consider weighting these areas' water identity on its own merits and letting the T1 score handle safety separately, rather than implicitly discounting the T2.4 because the area is "rough".

**7. The lido as a water feature.** Tooting Bec Lido, Brockwell Lido, Parliament Hill Lido, Hampstead Heath ponds — these count as meaningful water features in a way that distinguishes Tooting, Herne Hill, Gospel Oak from otherwise equivalent non-lido areas. Lidos are a distinctive London form of shared outdoor bathing water that residents use heavily in summer. Synthesis should flag lido-presence specifically.

**8. The River Wandle and the Ravensbourne are real watercourses that get ignored.** South-west London has the Wandle running from Wandsworth Town through Earlsfield to Colliers Wood; south-east London has the Ravensbourne running through Lewisham, Catford, and south. Both are real rivers with nature reserves, restoration projects, and walking paths, and both are entirely absent from the typical London "water identity" framing. Wandsworth Town, Earlsfield, Lewisham, Catford all get legitimate water credit from them.

**9. Masterplan-delivered parks are a distinct emerging category.** Claremont Park (Brent Cross Town), Cator Park (Kidbrooke Village), Tide (Greenwich Peninsula), Crossrail Place Roof Garden (Canary Wharf), Elephant Park (Elephant & Castle), Victory Park (East Village), future Earl's Court park — these are new, large, designed, and landscape-architect-led. They are structurally different from either Victorian parks or pocket urban squares. They tend to be exceptionally well-maintained in the first decade but depend on continued operator funding to stay that way. Synthesis should create a sub-category: "delivered by the masterplan" green, which is currently strong but has a maintenance-risk tail.

**10. North-west London is green-rich but water-poor; south-east London is the inverse.** The Hampstead Heath / Regent's Park / Primrose Hill / Queen's Park / Kensington Gardens corridor has some of the best green in London, but canal presence is concentrated at the south edge (Little Venice / Paddington Basin) and there is no river or dock. West Hampstead, Belsize Park, Kentish Town, Tufnell Park, Archway, Gospel Oak all score strong-to-exceptional on green and zero on water. South-east London is the inverse — dock-rich, canal-rare, and the green is spread across many medium parks. Synthesis can treat "NW green / SE water" as a rough regional pattern.

**11. Tideway works have temporarily reduced some Thames frontages.** Blackfriars, Chelsea Embankment, Vauxhall, Putney, and a few other areas had Thames Path interruptions and construction compounds through much of the late 2010s and early 2020s for the Tideway super-sewer. Most are now re-opened (2025) and the public realm is actually improved in many cases. Synthesis should not penalise these areas for legacy construction.

**12. The tidal Thames distinction matters.** The Thames is tidal all the way to Teddington Lock. This means that Wapping, Rotherhithe, Greenwich Peninsula, Woolwich, etc. have a Thames that rises and falls ~7m twice a day, exposing foreshore and changing water character throughout the day — meaningfully different from "still water at a dock basin". Synthesis could surface this as a flavour note — tidal river identity is distinctive and shapes what the water looks like (mud, boats grounded, bird activity).
